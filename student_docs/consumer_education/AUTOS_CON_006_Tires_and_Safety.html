Four Patches of Rubber

Your tires are the only part of your car that touches the road. Everything else is just along for the ride.

↓

Each tire contacts the road with about a 5”×5” patch of rubber — roughly the size of a postcard. Four postcards. That’s it. Those four small rectangles carry your entire vehicle, you, your passengers, and everything in the trunk. They determine whether you accelerate, turn, and — most critically — whether you stop in time.

Tires are the most underappreciated safety component on any vehicle. People obsess over horsepower, paint color, and infotainment screens, then roll around on rubber they’ve never inspected. This module changes that. By the end, you’ll know how to read a tire, judge its condition, choose the right type, and budget for replacements — because tire costs are part of the total cost of ownership you calculated in Module 5.

Reading a Tire Sidewall

Every tire prints its specs right on the side. Here’s how to decode them.

Look at the sidewall of any tire and you’ll see something like P215/65R16 95H. That string of numbers and letters tells you everything you need to know about what the tire is, what it fits, and what it can handle. Let’s break it down.

Why this matters: When you replace tires, the size must match what your vehicle requires. That information is on a placard inside the driver’s door jamb and in your owner’s manual. Using the wrong size affects speedometer accuracy, handling, braking, and can void your warranty.

The Penny Test and Tread Depth

Knowing when your tires are worn out could save your life.

New tires start with about 10/32” of tread depth. Oregon law requires a minimum of 2/32” — but by that point, your tires are already dangerous in wet conditions. Most safety experts recommend replacing tires at 4/32”, especially in the Pacific Northwest where rain is a constant.

The penny test: Insert a penny into the tread groove with Lincoln’s head pointing down. If you can see the top of Lincoln’s head, your tread is at or below 2/32” — replace immediately. For a better safety margin, use a quarter instead: if you can see the top of Washington’s head, you’re at 4/32” and should start shopping.

Every tire also has built-in tread wear indicators — small raised bars that sit between the tread grooves. When the tread surface wears down flush with these bars, the tire is legally worn out.

Tire Types

Not all rubber is created equal. Pick the type that matches your driving.

All-Season

The default on most new cars. Designed for a balance of dry, wet, and light snow performance. Good for Willamette Valley driving where winters are mild and rain is the primary concern.

Best for: Year-round Corvallis driving

Summer / Performance

Superior grip in warm, dry conditions and heavy rain. Compound hardens in cold temperatures, losing traction below ~45°F. Not suitable as a year-round tire in Oregon.

Best for: Warm-weather only vehicles

Winter / Snow

Softer rubber compound stays flexible below freezing. Aggressive tread pattern channels snow and slush. Look for the three-peak mountain snowflake (3PMSF) symbol. Required for mountain pass driving in Oregon.

Best for: Trips to Mt. Hood, Santiam Pass

All-Terrain

Thicker sidewalls and aggressive tread for mixed on-road/off-road use. Noisier on the highway and wear faster on pavement. Popular on trucks and SUVs in rural Oregon.

Best for: Gravel roads, light off-road
Oregon-specific tip: If you drive over the Coast Range or Cascades in winter, carry chains or have traction tires. ODOT can require chains on certain routes. All-season tires with the M+S (mud and snow) marking meet Oregon’s minimum traction tire requirement, but dedicated winter tires perform significantly better on ice and packed snow.

Tire Maintenance

A few simple habits make tires last longer and keep you safer.

Tire rotation — every 5,000 to 7,500 miles. Front tires wear faster because they handle steering and most of the braking force. Rotating tires (moving them to different positions) evens out the wear so all four tires last the same amount of time. Most shops charge $20–40, and many tire retailers include free rotation with purchase.

Alignment — check annually or after hitting a major pothole or curb. Misalignment causes uneven wear patterns (one edge wearing faster than the other) and makes the car pull to one side. An alignment typically costs $75–100.

Balancing — done whenever tires are mounted or when you feel vibration at highway speeds. Small weights are added to the rim to counterbalance imperfections. Usually included with tire installation.

Pressure checks — monthly, and before any long trip. Use the pressure listed on the driver’s door jamb placard, not the maximum pressure stamped on the tire sidewall. The sidewall number is the maximum the tire can hold safely — it’s not the recommended operating pressure for your vehicle.

Why Tire Pressure Matters

Underinflated tires increase rolling resistance, which wastes fuel — every 1 PSI below spec costs roughly 0.2% in fuel economy. They also run hotter, which accelerates wear and increases blowout risk. Overinflated tires ride harshly, wear unevenly in the center, and reduce the contact patch — meaning less grip when braking and cornering. Correct pressure saves money, extends tire life, and prevents blowouts.

Check the manufacture date. Every tire has a DOT code stamped on the sidewall ending in four digits — the first two are the week, the last two are the year. For example, “2319” means the 23rd week of 2019. Tires older than six years should be replaced regardless of tread depth — rubber compounds degrade over time, reducing grip and increasing blowout risk. Check all four tires, including the spare.

Don’t forget installation costs: mounting, balancing, valve stems, and disposal fees typically add $15–25 per tire ($60–100 for the set). Some retailers include installation when you buy from them — always ask.

The cheapest tires are rarely the best value. A $70 tire with a 40,000-mile warranty costs about $0.0018 per mile. A $130 tire with an 80,000-mile warranty costs about $0.0016 per mile — and performs better in wet braking the entire time. Look at the treadwear warranty (mileage rating) and divide to find the real cost per mile.

Check Yourself

Make sure you’ve got the concepts before you move on.

A tire reads P225/60R17 99T. What does the “60” mean, and what rim size does this tire fit?

The “60” is the aspect ratio — it means the sidewall height is 60% of the tire’s width (225 mm). So the sidewall is 135 mm tall. The tire fits a 17-inch rim (the number after the R). The “P” means passenger vehicle, “R” means radial construction, “99” is the load index, and “T” is the speed rating (up to 118 mph).

You do the penny test and can see the very top of Lincoln’s head on two of your tires. The other two look fine. What should you do?

Replace at least the two worn tires immediately — they’re at or below 2/32”, which is the legal minimum in Oregon and dangerously low for wet conditions. Ideally, replace all four for even wear and balanced handling. If you can only do two, put the new tires on the rear axle (even on a front-wheel-drive car) to prevent oversteer in wet conditions. Also investigate why two wore faster — you may need an alignment.

Your tire sidewall says “Max Press 51 PSI.” Your door jamb placard says 32 PSI. Which number do you use?

Use the door jamb placard: 32 PSI. The sidewall number is the maximum pressure the tire can safely hold — not the recommended operating pressure for your vehicle. Inflating to 51 PSI would cause harsh ride quality, uneven center wear, and a reduced contact patch. The vehicle manufacturer determined 32 PSI gives the best balance of handling, comfort, tread life, and fuel economy for your specific car.

Your friend says tire rotations are a scam to get you back in the shop. How do you respond?

Rotations are one of the cheapest ways to extend tire life. Front tires wear faster because they handle steering and most braking force. Without rotation, you’d replace fronts long before rears — wasting half the tread life on the rear tires. Rotating every 5,000–7,500 miles evens out wear so all four tires reach end-of-life together, saving you money. Many tire shops offer free rotation with purchase.

You’re planning a trip from Corvallis to Mt. Bachelor in January. You have all-season tires with the M+S marking. Are you prepared?

You meet the minimum Oregon traction tire requirement with M+S all-season tires, but you should carry chains as a backup. ODOT can require chains on Highway 20 over Santiam Pass and Highway 97 near Bend when conditions deteriorate. Dedicated winter tires (look for the 3PMSF snowflake symbol) would give you significantly better grip on ice and packed snow. Check TripCheck.com before you leave and carry chains regardless — conditions change fast in the Cascades.

Your Assignment: Tire Specifications

Research tires for your portfolio vehicle and build a maintenance plan.

Worked Example — 2019 Honda Civic LX

  • OEM tire size: P215/55R16 93H (from door jamb placard)
  • Selected tire: Continental TrueContact Tour — all-season
  • Price per tire: $132 · Set of 4: $528
  • Installation: $20/tire · Total: $80
  • Grand total: $608 installed
  • Treadwear warranty: 80,000 miles
  • Cost per mile: $608 ÷ 80,000 = $0.0076/mi
  • Rotation schedule: every 6,000 miles (with each oil change)
  1. Look up the correct tire size for your portfolio vehicle. Find it on the door jamb placard (or look it up in the owner’s manual or on TireRack.com by entering year/make/model).
  2. Research and select a specific tire (brand and model) in that size. Record:
    • Tire name and type (all-season, winter, etc.)
    • Price per tire and for a set of 4
    • Treadwear warranty (mileage)
    • Cost per mile (total cost ÷ warranty miles)
  3. Get an installation estimate. Call a local shop or check TireRack/Discount Tire online for mounting, balancing, and disposal fees. Add this to your total.
  4. Create a rotation schedule for your portfolio vehicle. Based on the manufacturer’s recommendation (typically 5,000–7,500 miles), note when the first four rotations would occur based on your estimated annual mileage.
  5. Submit your tire research to Canvas. This becomes Page 6 of your Vehicle Ownership Portfolio — Tire Specifications (size, type, cost, rotation schedule).

Choose tires you’d actually buy — not the cheapest and not the most expensive. Justify your choice with the cost-per-mile math.

  1. Four postcard-sized patches of rubber are the only thing between your car and the road. Inspect them monthly.
  2. The cheapest tire isn’t the best value — divide total cost by treadwear warranty miles to find real cost per mile.
  3. Use the door jamb placard for pressure, rotate every 5,000–7,500 miles, and replace at 4/32” tread depth for wet-weather safety.
