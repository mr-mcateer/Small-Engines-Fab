The Breakeven Point

Every car reaches a moment where fixing it costs more than it’s worth. Knowing when that is saves you from both sides of the mistake.

↓

There are two expensive mistakes in car ownership. The first is selling too early — dumping a car that still has years of reliable life left, eating thousands in unnecessary depreciation. The second is holding on too long — pouring repair money into a vehicle that’s worth less than what you’re spending to keep it alive.

The breakeven point is where those two curves cross. On one side, the car is still worth fixing — and you’ve got the maintenance records and trusted mechanic to make that work. On the other, every dollar you spend on repairs is a dollar you’ll never get back. Learning to identify that crossover — before you get there — is one of the most valuable financial skills a car owner can develop.

The Depreciation Curve

A $30,000 car doesn’t lose value at a steady rate. The curve is steep, then flat, then nearly invisible.

Year 1

$23,400

Lost $6,600 (22%)

Year 3

$17,100

Lost $12,900 (43%)

Year 5

$13,200

Lost $16,800 (56%)

Year 7

$10,500

Lost $19,500 (65%)

Year 10: ~$7,500 — lost 75% total

Notice the pattern: the car loses almost half its value in the first three years, then the losses slow dramatically. Between years 7 and 10, it only drops another $3,000. This is why the sweet spot of ownership — buying at 2–3 years old and selling at 7–8 years — captures the flattest part of the curve. You let someone else absorb the steepest depreciation, and you sell before the car becomes a maintenance liability.

The ownership sweet spot: Buy at 2–3 years old when the steepest depreciation is already behind it. Sell at 7–8 years before major age-related repairs begin stacking up. You ride the flattest part of the curve — lowest cost per year of ownership.

Repair vs. Replace: The Decision

When does fixing it stop making sense? There are two rules.

The Single-Repair Rule

50% If one repair exceeds 50% of the car’s current value, it’s time to consider selling.

The Annual-Cost Rule

12 mo If total annual repairs exceed 12 months of payments on a replacement, consider selling.

Worked Example — Should You Fix It?

  • Your car’s KBB value: $6,000
  • Transmission repair quote: $3,500
  • $3,500 ÷ $6,000 = 58% of car’s value
  • That’s above 50% — consider selling
  •  
  • Alternative: replacement car payment = $250/mo
  • $250 × 12 = $3,000/year in payments
  • This year you’ve already spent $1,200 on other repairs
  • $1,200 + $3,500 = $4,700 in annual repair costs
  • $4,700 > $3,000 — the annual-cost rule also says sell

These rules aren’t absolute — if the transmission fix would give you three more reliable years, the math might still work. But when both rules point toward selling, the numbers are clear. The hardest part is overcoming the emotional attachment. You know the car. You’ve maintained it. But sentimentality is not a financial strategy.

Exception worth noting: If the car has very low miles for its age, only needs one major fix, and everything else is solid, the repair can still make sense even above 50%. The rules are guidelines, not laws — but they keep emotion out of the math.

How to Sell Your Car

Three paths, each with different tradeoffs between money and effort.

Private Party

MoneyMost — 15–25% more than trade-in
EffortHigh — photos, listings, showings, negotiation
TimelineDays to weeks

Trade-In

MoneyLeast — wholesale value
EffortLow — drive in, drive out in new car

Online (Carvana, CarMax)

MoneyMiddle — instant offer, no haggling

Best forQuick sale without dealer pressure

To determine your asking price for a private sale, use KBB’s “Private Party Value” — not dealer retail, not trade-in. Price slightly above that to leave room for negotiation. Before listing, prepare the car: wash and detail inside and out, gather all maintenance records, and fix small cosmetic issues (burned-out bulbs, torn floor mats). A clean car with records sells faster and for more money.

Oregon title transfer: Both buyer and seller sign the title. The seller fills in the odometer reading and sale price. The buyer takes the signed title to the DMV within 30 days to register the vehicle. There is no state sales tax in Oregon, but there is a title and registration fee. Keep a copy of the signed title for your records.

Selling Safely

You’re advertising that you have something valuable and you’re willing to meet a stranger. Be smart about it.

Private Sale Safety Rules

Meet in public. Police station parking lots are ideal — many departments designate safe transaction zones with cameras. Never invite strangers to your home.

Cash or cashier’s check only. No personal checks, no payment apps for large amounts, no “I’ll send the rest later.” Verify cashier’s checks with the issuing bank before signing over the title.

Never let the buyer test drive alone. You ride along, you hold their driver’s license, and you confirm they have valid insurance before they get behind the wheel.

Bill of sale protects both parties. Include both names, date, VIN, odometer reading, sale price, and the phrase “sold as-is.” Both parties sign. Both parties keep a copy. This document proves you no longer own the car if something happens after the sale.

Bring someone with you. Two people are always safer than one when meeting a stranger for a cash transaction. Let someone else know where you’re going, who you’re meeting, and when you expect to be back.

Check Yourself

Work through these scenarios before moving on.

Your car is worth $8,000. The mechanic quotes $2,800 for a new catalytic converter. Should you fix it or sell?

Fix it. $2,800 is 35% of the car’s value — below the 50% threshold. If the rest of the car is solid and you haven’t been spending heavily on other repairs this year, this one fix is worth doing. A catalytic converter replacement doesn’t signal broader mechanical failure.

You’ve spent $1,800 on repairs in the last six months. Now the AC compressor fails — another $1,400. The car is worth $5,500. What do you do?

Strongly consider selling. The single repair ($1,400) is only 25% of value, which passes the first test. But your projected annual repair cost is $3,200+ for the first six months alone, and a replacement car payment of $250/month would only cost $3,000/year. When the annual-cost rule fails and repairs are accelerating, the trend is your real signal.

Your friend bought a brand-new car and wants to sell it after 18 months because they’re “bored with it.” Good idea?

Terrible idea. At 18 months, the car has lost roughly 25–30% of its original value. If they financed it, they’re almost certainly underwater — owing more than the car is worth. Selling now means paying out of pocket just to close the loan. This is the “selling too early” mistake. Unless there’s a genuine financial emergency, they should keep the car until at least year 5–6.

A 10-year-old truck with 180,000 miles needs a $1,500 brake job. KBB says it’s worth $4,000. Fix or sell?

Fix it. $1,500 is 37.5% of value — below 50%. Brakes are normal wear items, not a sign of mechanical failure. If the engine and transmission are healthy, a $4,000 truck with new brakes could easily give you two more years of service. That’s $2,000/year in total vehicle cost — far cheaper than any replacement.

You’re selling your car privately for $7,000. A buyer wants to pay with Venmo and pick it up tomorrow. What do you do?

Decline the payment method. Venmo and similar apps offer limited protection for large transactions and can be reversed. Insist on cash or a verified cashier’s check. Meet at a police station safe-trade zone, bring someone with you, and don’t sign over the title until the funds are confirmed in hand.

Your Assignment: Long-Term Ownership Plan

Project the future of your portfolio vehicle and identify the optimal exit window.

Worked Example — Depreciation Projection

  • Portfolio vehicle: purchased at $18,000 (3 years old)
  • Year 1 of ownership (car age 4): $15,300 — lost $2,700 (15%)
  • Year 3 of ownership (car age 6): $12,100 — lost $5,900 total
  • Year 5 of ownership (car age 8): $9,700 — lost $8,300 total
  • Year 7 of ownership (car age 10): $8,100 — lost $9,900 total
  • Optimal sell window: years 4–5 of ownership (car age 7–8)
  • $2,000 repair would exceed 50% of value at: ~$4,000 car value (around year 8–9 of ownership)
  1. Using your portfolio vehicle from Module 1, look up its current KBB Private Party Value. Record this as your starting point.
  2. Project the vehicle’s value at years 1, 3, 5, 7, and 10 from now. Use KBB’s depreciation data or the average depreciation rates from this module.
    • Year 1: 15% loss
    • Year 3: 10–12% per year
    • Years 5–7: 7–9% per year
    • Years 8+: 5–7% per year
  3. Identify your optimal ownership window — the range of years where the car still holds reasonable value but before major age-related repairs are likely.
  4. Calculate: at what future value would a $2,000 repair exceed 50% of the car’s worth? How many years from now is that?
  5. Write a one-paragraph summary explaining your long-term plan: how long you’d keep this vehicle, when you’d sell, and how you’d sell it (private party, trade-in, or online).
  6. Submit to Canvas. This becomes Page 14 of your Vehicle Ownership Portfolio — Long-Term Plan (depreciation curve, optimal ownership window).
  1. The breakeven point is real — learn to see it before you reach it, and you’ll avoid the two most expensive ownership mistakes.
  2. Use the 50% single-repair rule and the annual-cost rule to take emotion out of the repair-vs-replace decision.
  3. When you sell, preparation and safety matter as much as price — clean the car, gather records, meet in public, and accept only verified payment.
