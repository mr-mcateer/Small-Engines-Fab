You’re Required to Buy It. You Should Understand It.

Auto insurance is mandatory. Understanding it is optional — and that’s where most people lose money.

↓

The Law Says You Must

Insurance isn’t optional. But understanding it is — and that’s the problem.

Oregon requires every driver to carry liability insurance. The state minimum is 25/50/20 — that’s $25,000 for bodily injury per person, $50,000 per accident, and $20,000 for property damage. Those numbers sound big until you realize a single trip to the ER can cost $50,000+, and a new truck bumper runs $5,000. Minimums are a floor, not a recommendation.

Get caught driving without insurance in Oregon and you’re looking at a license suspension, fines, and an SR-22 filing requirement — which is basically a flag on your record that tells the state your insurance company is watching you. That SR-22 stays with you for three years and makes your premiums significantly more expensive.

Here’s the reframe: you’re not throwing money away. You’re buying protection against a financial disaster that could follow you for decades. A single at-fault accident without insurance can result in a lawsuit, wage garnishment, and a wrecked credit score before you’re 20 years old.

What the Coverage Types Actually Mean

Four types of coverage. Each one protects something different.

Liability

Pays for damage you cause to other people and their property. This is the coverage Oregon requires. It does NOT cover your car — only theirs. If you rear-end someone, liability pays for their bumper, their medical bills, and their lawyer. Your car? That’s on you.

Required by law · 25/50/20 minimum in OR

Collision

Pays to fix your car after an accident, regardless of who was at fault. You hit a pole? Collision. Someone hits you and drives off? Collision. This is technically optional — but if you have a car loan, your lender will require it.

Optional · Required by lenders

Comprehensive

Pays for damage to your car from everything except collisions: theft, hail, fire, hitting a deer, falling tree branches, vandalism, a shopping cart rolling into your door. If it wasn’t a crash, it’s comprehensive.

Optional · Required by lenders

Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM)

Pays for your injuries when the other driver has no insurance or not enough to cover your costs. Oregon requires UM/UIM coverage matching your liability limits — 25/50 minimum. This is your backup plan for other people’s bad decisions.

Required in OR · Must match liability limits

The simple version: Liability = their stuff. Collision = your car in a crash. Comprehensive = your car from everything else. UM/UIM = you, when they don’t have insurance.

The Deductible Trade-Off

Lower deductible = higher premium. Higher deductible = lower premium. The math matters.

Your deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before insurance kicks in. If you have a $1,000 deductible and your repair costs $4,000, you pay $1,000 and insurance covers $3,000. Choosing your deductible is a bet on how likely you are to file a claim.

$250 Deductible

~$140/mo Lower out-of-pocket if you file a claim $1,680/year in premiums

$1,000 Deductible

~$105/mo Higher out-of-pocket if you file a claim $1,260/year in premiums
$420 saved per year with the higher deductible

But you pay $750 more out of pocket if you file a claim

The decision rule: If you can afford to cover $1,000 in an emergency, the higher deductible saves money over time. If you can’t, the lower deductible is insurance for your insurance. Either way, know the trade-off before you choose.

Why Young Drivers Pay More

Age is the single biggest factor in what you pay. Here’s why — and what you can control.

Insurance companies price risk. Statistically, drivers aged 16–19 are involved in more accidents per mile driven than any other age group. That’s not an opinion — it’s actuarial data built from millions of claims. The result: a 17-year-old in Oregon pays roughly $3,500–$5,000 per year for their own policy. Added to a parent’s policy, that drops to roughly $1,500–$2,500 per year — still significant, but much more manageable.

Credit score matters in most states. Insurance companies in 47 states (including Oregon) can use your credit-based insurance score to set premiums. A lower credit score often means a higher premium — one more reason your financial habits in your late teens matter more than you think.

How to Save on Insurance

You can’t change your age. But you can control almost everything else.

Your Premium Defense Playbook

Stay on your parent’s policy as long as possible. This is the single biggest discount available to you. Being added to an existing policy is dramatically cheaper than starting your own.

Earn the good student discount. Most insurers offer 5–15% off for maintaining a GPA of 3.0 or higher. Bring your transcript or report card — it’s free money.

Take a defensive driving course. Oregon-approved courses can knock another 5–10% off your premium, and you’ll be a better driver in the process.

Bundle policies. If your family bundles auto with homeowner’s or renter’s insurance, the multi-policy discount typically saves 5–15%.

Raise your deductible — if you can cover the cost. Moving from $500 to $1,000 can save 15–30% on collision and comprehensive premiums.

Shop around. Get quotes from at least three companies. Rates vary dramatically — the same driver can see quotes that differ by $1,000+ per year between companies.

Ask about telematics programs. Programs like Snapshot, Drive Safe & Save, or similar usage-based options track your driving habits and can offer discounts for safe driving. Read the fine print — some can also raise your rate if your driving is poor.

Stack the discounts. A young driver on a parent’s policy with good student + defensive driving + bundling + higher deductible can realistically save 40–50% compared to a standalone policy with default settings. That’s potentially $1,500–$2,000 per year back in your pocket.

Check Yourself

Make sure you’ve got the concepts before you move on.

You rear-end someone at a stop light. Which coverage pays for the damage to their car?

Liability. Your liability coverage pays for damage you cause to other people and their property. It does not cover your own vehicle — only theirs. This is the coverage Oregon requires you to carry.

A tree falls on your car during a storm. Which coverage applies?

Comprehensive. Falling objects, weather damage, theft, fire, animal strikes, and vandalism are all covered under comprehensive — not collision. Collision only applies when your car hits (or is hit by) another vehicle or object while driving.

Your friend says they only carry liability because it’s cheapest. They drive a $15,000 car with a loan. What’s wrong with this plan?

Their lender almost certainly requires collision and comprehensive coverage. If they’re only carrying liability, they’re likely violating their loan agreement. Worse: if the car is totaled, they still owe the full loan balance with no insurance payout to cover it. They’d be making payments on a car that no longer exists.

You’re comparing two policies: one at $120/month with a $500 deductible, another at $95/month with a $1,000 deductible. Over 2 years with no claims, which costs less total?

The $95/month policy. $95 × 24 months = $2,280. $120 × 24 months = $2,880. The lower-premium policy saves you $600 over two claim-free years. The trade-off: if you do file a claim, you’ll pay $500 more out of pocket with the higher deductible. But over two years of no claims, the savings far outweigh the risk.

Someone without insurance hits your car. Who pays?

Your Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM) coverage. This is exactly why Oregon requires it. UM/UIM protects you when the other driver has no insurance or doesn’t have enough to cover your injuries and damages. Without it, you’d be left paying your own medical bills out of pocket — or suing someone who likely can’t pay.
