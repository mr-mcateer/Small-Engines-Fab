The Drivetrain Is Changing

Electric, hybrid, plug-in hybrid, hydrogen — the future of driving isn’t coming. It’s here. Understanding the ownership math is your job.

↓

The internal combustion engine dominated personal transportation for over a century. That’s changing fast. Every major automaker now sells at least one electric or hybrid model, and many are phasing out gas-only vehicles entirely within the next decade.

Whether you end up buying gas, hybrid, or electric, you need to understand how each drivetrain option affects your total cost of ownership. This isn’t about politics or picking a side. It’s about math — the same ownership math you’ve been building all semester. The drivetrain you choose changes your fuel costs, maintenance schedule, insurance rates, resale value, and daily convenience. Your job is to run the numbers for your specific situation.

Four Drivetrains, Four Trade-Offs

Each one changes how you fuel, maintain, and budget for your vehicle.

ICE — Internal Combustion

Gas or diesel engine. The technology you already know. Established fueling infrastructure everywhere. Higher fuel costs per mile. Regular oil changes, transmission service, exhaust system maintenance. Widest selection of vehicles and price points on the used market.

25–35 MPG typical sedan · $3.50–4.50/gal

HEV — Hybrid

Gas engine paired with a small electric motor. No plugging in required — the battery charges through regenerative braking and the gas engine. Better fuel economy in city driving. Still needs gas, still needs oil changes, but uses less fuel overall.

40–55 MPG typical · No charging needed

PHEV — Plug-In Hybrid

Larger battery than an HEV with a plug-in charger. Electric-only for short trips (20–50 miles), then switches to gas for longer drives. Best of both worlds if your commute is short. Complexity of two systems to maintain. Heavier than both ICE and HEV.

20–50 mi EV range · 30–40 MPG on gas

BEV — Battery Electric

No gas engine at all. Powered entirely by a battery pack and electric motor(s). No oil changes, no transmission fluid, no exhaust system. Lower per-mile fuel cost. Requires access to charging. Higher purchase price. Range varies by model and conditions.

200–300+ mi range · $0.04–0.05/mi electricity
No single drivetrain is “best.” Each one involves trade-offs between purchase price, fuel cost, maintenance cost, convenience, and infrastructure access. The right choice depends on your budget, driving habits, and where you live. Your job is to calculate — not assume.

The 5-Year TCO Comparison

Same category sedan. Different drivetrains. Let the math talk.

Gas Sedan (ICE)

$39,500 Purchase: $28,000 Fuel: $1,800/yr × 5 = $9,000 Maintenance: $1,200/yr × 5 = $6,000 Insurance: $1,500/yr × 5 = $7,500 Resale after 5 yr: −$11,000 Incentives: $0

Electric Sedan (BEV)

$30,000 Purchase: $35,000 Electricity: $600/yr × 5 = $3,000 Maintenance: $600/yr × 5 = $3,000 Insurance: $1,700/yr × 5 = $8,500 Resale after 5 yr: −$12,000 Incentives: −$7,500
$9,500 lower TCO for the EV over 5 years

Based on national averages. Your numbers will vary by location, driving habits, electricity rates, and available incentives. Always run your own calculation.

The EV costs $7,000 more upfront but saves money on fuel and maintenance every year. Federal incentives and stronger resale narrow the gap — and then some. However, the math shifts if you can’t charge at home, if electricity costs more in your area, or if incentives don’t apply to the model you’re considering. The gas sedan has lower insurance and a simpler ownership experience in areas without robust charging infrastructure.

Why EV maintenance costs less: No oil changes. No transmission fluid. No timing belt. No exhaust system. Regenerative braking reduces brake pad wear significantly. The main long-term maintenance concern is battery health — most EV batteries are warrantied for 8 years or 100,000 miles.

Range anxiety — the fear of running out of charge — is the most common concern people raise about EVs. It’s real, but it’s diminishing. Most Americans drive fewer than 40 miles per day, well within the range of any modern EV. The challenge is less about range and more about access: do you have a garage or driveway where you can charge overnight? If you rent an apartment without dedicated parking, home charging may not be an option, and relying on public chargers adds cost and inconvenience.

Oregon electricity costs average around $0.11/kWh — among the lowest in the nation thanks to hydroelectric power. At that rate, charging a typical EV costs roughly $0.03–0.04 per mile driven. A gas car getting 30 MPG at $4.00/gallon costs about $0.13 per mile. That’s a 3–4x difference in fuel cost per mile.

Incentives, Drawbacks, and Real-World Factors

The fine print matters as much as the sticker price.

What Works in Your Favor

Federal tax credit: up to $7,500 for qualifying new EVs. Income limits and vehicle price caps apply.

Used EV credit: up to $4,000 for qualifying used EVs purchased from a dealer.

Oregon Clean Vehicle Rebate: additional state-level incentive for qualifying buyers.

Lower fuel & maintenance costs compound over years of ownership.

What Works Against You

Battery replacement: $5,000–$15,000 if needed outside warranty. Most batteries are warrantied 8 years/100K miles.

Insurance: EV insurance can run 10–25% higher due to repair costs and parts availability.

Apartment living: no garage means no Level 2 home charging. Public chargers cost more and take time.

Cold weather: EV range drops 20–40% in freezing temperatures due to battery chemistry and cabin heating.

Run Your Own Numbers

National averages are useful for comparison, but they don’t tell you what your ownership will cost. Your electricity rate, daily commute distance, access to home charging, insurance quotes, and available incentives are all specific to you. Before deciding between gas and electric, build the spreadsheet for your actual situation. The Department of Energy’s fueleconomy.gov has calculators that can help.

Check Yourself

Work through these before you move on.

You drive 12,000 miles per year. Gas costs $4.00/gal and your car gets 30 MPG. Electricity costs $0.11/kWh and an EV uses 0.30 kWh/mile. What’s the annual fuel cost difference?

Gas: 12,000 ÷ 30 = 400 gallons × $4.00 = $1,600/year. Electric: 12,000 × 0.30 kWh = 3,600 kWh × $0.11 = $396/year. Difference: $1,204/year saved on fuel with the EV. Over 5 years, that’s roughly $6,000 in fuel savings alone.

A new EV costs $36,000 and qualifies for a $7,500 federal tax credit. A comparable gas sedan costs $27,000. After the credit, what’s the effective price gap?

EV after credit: $36,000 − $7,500 = $28,500. Gas sedan: $27,000. Effective gap: $1,500. That gap can be closed within 1–2 years through lower fuel and maintenance costs. But remember: the tax credit is applied when you file your taxes, not at the point of sale (unless the dealer offers it upfront as a point-of-sale discount). You still need to finance or pay the full purchase price initially.

Your apartment complex has no EV chargers and you park on the street. Should you buy a BEV?

Probably not — at least not yet. Without reliable home charging, you’d depend on public chargers, which are more expensive ($0.30–0.60/kWh vs. $0.11 at home) and less convenient. A hybrid (HEV) gives you better fuel economy without needing to plug in. A PHEV could work if there’s a Level 2 charger at your workplace. Practicality matters as much as sticker price.

Your friend says EVs are “maintenance-free.” Is that accurate?

No. EVs have lower maintenance, not zero maintenance. You still need tire rotations, cabin air filter changes, brake fluid service, windshield wipers, and suspension components. Battery coolant needs periodic replacement on many models. The big savings come from eliminating oil changes, transmission service, spark plugs, timing belts, and exhaust system repairs. “Lower maintenance” is accurate. “Maintenance-free” is not.

It’s 15°F outside and your EV is rated at 280 miles of range. How far can you realistically expect to drive?

Cold weather can reduce EV range by 20–40%. At the conservative end (40% reduction): 280 × 0.60 = 168 miles. At the moderate end (20% reduction): 280 × 0.80 = 224 miles. Plan for 170–220 miles of real-world range in very cold conditions. This matters for road trips and for daily driving if your commute is long. Preconditioning the battery while plugged in helps reduce the loss.

Your Assignment: EV vs. Gas TCO Comparison

Put your portfolio vehicle head-to-head against an electric or hybrid equivalent.

Worked Example — 5-Year TCO Comparison

  • Portfolio vehicle: 2022 Honda Civic (gas) — $22,000 purchase
  • EV equivalent: 2022 Chevrolet Bolt EUV — $21,000 used (after $4,000 used EV credit)
  • Gas fuel: 12,000 mi/yr ÷ 33 MPG × $4.00/gal = $1,455/yr
  • EV electricity: 12,000 mi/yr × 0.29 kWh/mi × $0.11/kWh = $383/yr
  • Gas maintenance: $1,100/yr · EV maintenance: $550/yr
  • Gas insurance: $1,400/yr · EV insurance: $1,600/yr
  • 5-yr gas TCO: $22,000 + $7,275 + $5,500 + $7,000 − $12,500 resale = $29,275
  • 5-yr EV TCO: $21,000 + $1,915 + $2,750 + $8,000 − $10,500 resale = $23,165
  • EV saves ~$6,100 over 5 years in this scenario
  1. Identify your portfolio vehicle (the gas car you’ve been researching all semester). Record its purchase price, MPG, and estimated annual maintenance cost.
  2. Find an electric or hybrid equivalent in the same vehicle category. Use the same research sources (KBB, Edmunds, fueleconomy.gov) to get purchase price, efficiency rating, and maintenance estimates.
  3. Build a 5-year TCO comparison that includes:
    • Purchase price (after any applicable tax credits or incentives)
    • Annual fuel or electricity cost (show your math)
    • Annual maintenance cost
    • Annual insurance cost (estimate or quote)
    • Estimated resale value after 5 years
  4. Answer these questions in 2–3 paragraphs: Which vehicle costs less over 5 years? Is the EV or hybrid practical for your living situation (home charging access, daily commute distance, climate)? What would need to change for the other option to make more sense?
  5. Submit to Canvas. This becomes Page 15 of your Vehicle Ownership Portfolio — Future Vehicle Tech (EV/hybrid TCO comparison).

Be honest in your analysis. If the gas car makes more financial sense for your situation, say so. If the EV wins, show why. The point is the math, not the conclusion.

  1. Every drivetrain — ICE, hybrid, PHEV, BEV — has trade-offs. The right choice depends on your numbers, not anyone else’s opinion.
  2. EVs cost less to fuel and maintain, but more to purchase and insure. Run the 5-year TCO before deciding.
  3. Charging access is the practical gatekeeper. If you can’t charge at home, factor public charging costs and convenience into your math.
