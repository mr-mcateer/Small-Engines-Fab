Stranded Is a Plan You Didn’t Make

Everyone breaks down eventually. The question is whether you’re prepared when it happens.

↓

It’s not a question of if — it’s a question of when. Flat tire, dead battery, overheating engine, empty fuel tank. At some point in your driving life, your car will stop working when you need it most. That’s not pessimism — it’s statistics.

The difference between a 20-minute inconvenience and a genuinely dangerous situation is preparation. A driver who has a kit, knows the steps, and stays calm turns a breakdown into a story. A driver who doesn’t can end up stranded on a dark highway shoulder with no flashlight, no phone charge, and no plan.

This module gives you the plan.

The Emergency Kit

What every car should carry — before you need any of it.

You don’t build an emergency kit during an emergency. You build it on a calm Saturday afternoon and toss it in the trunk. Most of these items cost under $10 each. The entire kit can be assembled for $50–80 and lasts for years.

Safety Items

VisibilityReflective triangles or flares
LightFlashlight + extra batteries
MedicalBasic first aid kit
CommunicationPhone charger (12V adapter)

Basic Tools

Comfort & Survival

WarmthBlanket (wool or emergency)
HydrationBottled water
FoodGranola bars or shelf-stable snacks
WeatherRain poncho
Oregon drivers take note: Rain, mountain passes, and rural two-lane highways mean a breakdown here can leave you cold, wet, and far from help. The blanket, water, and rain poncho aren’t optional — they’re essential.

Flat Tire Response

The most common roadside emergency — and the most fixable.

A flat tire is almost never dangerous by itself. It becomes dangerous when the driver panics, pulls over in the wrong spot, or doesn’t know the procedure. Here’s the correct sequence:

Pull over safely — away from traffic, flat & firm surface
↓
Hazard lights ON · Parking brake SET
↓
Loosen lug nuts BEFORE jacking (1/4 turn each)
↓
Jack at the vehicle’s designated jack point
↓
Remove lugs, swap tire, hand-tighten in star pattern
↓
Lower vehicle, final torque in star pattern
↓
Drive to a tire shop — spare is temporary
The spare tire is not a regular tire. Most compact spares (donuts) are rated for 50 mph maximum and 50 miles maximum. Don’t merge onto the freeway and drive across the state on a spare. Get to a tire shop and get the real tire repaired or replaced.

Practice Before You Need It

Change a tire in your driveway on a calm day. Find the jack, find the jack points, practice loosening the lugs. The worst time to learn a new skill is on a dark highway shoulder in the rain. If you can change a tire at home in 15 minutes, you can do it anywhere.

Dead Battery & Jump Starting

Four cables, two batteries, one specific order.

A dead battery usually means you left something on — headlights, dome light, phone charger — or the battery is old and failed. Jump starting gets you moving again, but the order of cable connections matters. Get it wrong and you risk sparks near a battery that produces hydrogen gas.

RED cable → DEAD battery positive (+)
↓
RED cable → GOOD battery positive (+)
↓
BLACK cable → GOOD battery negative (−)
↓
BLACK cable → UNPAINTED METAL on dead car (not the battery!)
↓
Run the good car for 2–3 minutes
↓
Start the dead car · Remove cables in REVERSE order
Why unpainted metal instead of the negative terminal? Connecting the last cable can produce a small spark. You want that spark far away from the battery, which can vent hydrogen gas. An engine bolt, bracket, or strut tower bolt works. This isn’t optional — it’s a safety step.
Never jump a damaged battery. If the battery case is swollen, cracked, leaking fluid, or smells like rotten eggs (sulfur), do not attempt a jump start. A damaged battery can explode. Call for a tow.

Overheating & Other Scenarios

Different emergencies, same principle: stay calm, follow a procedure.

Engine Overheating

The temperature gauge is climbing into the red. Steam might be visible from under the hood. Here’s what to do — and what not to do:

Do This

Pull over as soon as safely possible. Turn OFF the A/C immediately. Turn ON the heater to full blast — the heater core acts as a second radiator and pulls heat away from the engine. Let the engine idle for a few minutes, then shut it off. You can pop the hood to help heat escape, but do NOT open the radiator cap — the system is pressurized above 200°F and will spray boiling coolant. Wait at least 30 minutes before touching the cap.

Never Do This

Do NOT open the radiator cap when the engine is hot. The cooling system is pressurized and the coolant is above 200°F. Removing the cap releases a geyser of boiling coolant that can cause severe burns to your face, hands, and arms. Wait until the engine is cool to the touch.

Accident Response

If you’re involved in a collision, the priority sequence is: people, safety, documentation, information.

  1. Check yourself and passengers for injuries
  2. If the vehicle can be moved safely, pull to the shoulder or a parking lot
  3. Call 911 if there are injuries, the road is blocked, or you’re unsure
  4. Exchange insurance information, driver’s license numbers, and phone numbers
  5. Document everything with photos: damage to all vehicles, the scene, license plates, road conditions

Running Out of Fuel

It happens to more people than you’d think. When the engine starts sputtering: pull as far to the right as possible, activate your hazard lights, and stay in the vehicle if you’re on a highway. Call for help or use a roadside assistance app. Walking along a highway is far more dangerous than waiting in your car.

When to Call for Help

Knowing your limits is not weakness — it’s judgment.

Not every roadside situation is a DIY fix. Sometimes the smartest move is to stay in your locked car with hazards on and make a phone call. Here are the situations where calling beats doing:

Too Dangerous to DIY

Highway shoulder with fast traffic. Dark or poorly lit conditions. Bad weather — rain, ice, snow. You’re in an unfamiliar area. You don’t have the right tools or knowledge for the repair.

Who to Call

Your auto insurance roadside assistance (check if it’s included or add it for ~$2–5/month). AAA membership. Your vehicle manufacturer’s roadside program (many include 3–5 years of coverage with new cars). In Oregon, dial *677 for highway assistance.

There is no shame in calling for help. Professional drivers, mechanics, and emergency responders all call for tows when the situation calls for it. Your safety is always worth more than your pride. A tow costs $75–150. A hospital visit costs thousands. The math is simple.

Check Yourself

Make sure you’ve got the procedures down before you need them.

You get a flat tire on a slight hill. What’s the first thing you do before grabbing the jack?

Set the parking brake and loosen the lug nuts. On a hill, you also want to chock the wheels that will stay on the ground (a rock or piece of wood behind the tire works). Loosening the lugs while the car is still on the ground gives you leverage — if you try it while the car is jacked up, the wheel just spins. Then and only then do you place the jack at the designated jack point.

When jump starting a car, where does the last black (negative) cable go — and why?

Unpainted metal on the dead car’s engine block — not the negative battery terminal. The last connection can create a spark. Batteries vent hydrogen gas, which is flammable. By clamping to a bracket or bolt away from the battery, any spark is safely distant from the gas. An engine bolt, strut tower, or alternator bracket all work.

Your temperature gauge is pegged in the red and steam is coming from under the hood. You pull over. What do you do next?

Turn off the A/C and turn the heater to full blast. This pulls heat away from the engine through the heater core. Let it idle briefly, then shut off the engine. You can pop the hood to help it cool, but do NOT open the radiator cap — the system is pressurized and the coolant is above 200°F. Wait at least 30 minutes before touching the cap.

You’re driving on I-5 at night and your car dies. Should you get out and try to diagnose the problem?

No. Stay in the vehicle with your seatbelt on, hazard lights activated, and call for help. A dark highway shoulder is one of the most dangerous places to stand. Other drivers may not see you. Professional roadside assistance has flashing lights, reflective gear, and training for highway work. Your safety is worth more than a faster fix.

Your friend says roadside assistance is a waste of money. What’s your response?

It costs $2–5/month through most insurance companies — less than a single coffee per week. One tow without coverage costs $75–150+, and that’s just for a short distance. If you break down 30 miles from home, an uninsured tow can easily exceed $200. It pays for itself the first time you use it. Many new cars include manufacturer roadside assistance free for 3–5 years — check your owner’s manual.

Your Assignment: Emergency Preparedness Plan

Build your kit on paper first, then build it for real.

Example — Kit Item Entry

  • Item: NOCO Boost Plus GB40 jump starter
  • Source: Amazon
  • Price: $99.95
  • Why: Eliminates the need for a second car to jump start — works solo
  • Replaces: Traditional jumper cables (still works but requires another vehicle)
  1. Build your personal emergency kit list. Research actual products and current prices from Amazon, AutoZone, O’Reilly, or any auto parts retailer. Include at least 10 items covering all three categories (safety, tools, comfort/survival). For each item, list the product name, source, price, and a one-sentence explanation of why you chose it.
  2. Calculate your total kit cost. Add up every item. Most complete kits should fall between $50 and $200. If yours exceeds $200, identify which items could be replaced with cheaper alternatives.
  3. Write response procedures for three scenarios:
    • Flat tire on a two-lane road
    • Dead battery in a parking lot
    • You’re involved in a minor fender-bender at an intersection
    For each scenario, write the step-by-step procedure in your own words. Include what to do first, what tools or kit items you’d use, and when you’d call for help instead of handling it yourself.
  4. Submit your kit list and three response procedures to Canvas. This becomes Page 10 of your Vehicle Ownership Portfolio — Emergency Preparedness (kit list, response procedures).

This isn’t hypothetical. Once you’ve written the list, go buy the items and put them in your car. The assignment gets you a grade. The actual kit might get you home safely.

  1. Every car needs an emergency kit — build it before you need it, not during the emergency.
  2. The procedures for flat tires, dead batteries, and overheating all have a specific order — learn them now.
  3. Calling for help is a valid response — your safety is always worth more than a faster fix.
