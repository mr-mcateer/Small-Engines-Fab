Everything on the Sticker They Hope You Won’t Read

The window sticker is your single best research tool. Learn to read it.

The Monroney Sticker Exists Because of Federal Law

Before 1958, car buyers had no way to know what a fair price looked like.

In 1958, Senator A.S. “Mike” Monroney of Oklahoma pushed through the Automobile Information Disclosure Act. The law requires every new car sold in the United States to display a standardized price sticker on the window — visible to every buyer, with every cost itemized. Before this law, dealers could charge whatever they wanted, and buyers had no baseline for comparison.

The Monroney sticker is not a suggestion. It is a federal requirement. The dealer cannot alter it or remove it before the sale. It shows the manufacturer’s suggested retail price, every factory-installed option, the destination charge, and the EPA fuel economy estimates. It is your starting point — not the dealer’s asking price, not a take-it-or-leave-it number, but the baseline from which every negotiation begins.

The Monroney sticker is required by federal law. It cannot be altered by the dealer. Everything else they add — those are suggestions.

Anatomy of the Sticker

Every section of the Monroney sticker tells you something specific. Here’s what to look for.

Base Price

The manufacturer’s starting price for this trim level. Includes standard equipment — the engine, transmission, base audio system, standard safety features, and everything that comes on the car before you add a single option.

This is not what you’ll pay. It’s where the math begins.

Optional Equipment / Packages

Technology packages, premium audio, sunroof, heated seats, advanced driver-assist systems — anything added at the factory beyond the standard trim. Each has a listed price.

Know which options matter to you before you shop. Dealers stock specific configurations. If you walk in without a list, you’ll pay for features you never wanted.

Destination & Delivery Charge

The cost to ship the car from the factory to the dealer. This is a real cost — vehicles are transported by rail and truck from assembly plants across the country or overseas.

This fee is the same for every dealer selling this model. It is non-negotiable and set by the manufacturer.

Total MSRP

Base Price + Options + Destination = the Manufacturer’s Suggested Retail Price. The keyword is SUGGESTED.

This is not what you should pay. It is the ceiling. In most market conditions, buyers pay at or below MSRP. The only time you should consider paying above MSRP is when demand dramatically exceeds supply — and even then, shop around.

Important distinction: Anything labeled “dealer-installed accessories” or “dealer markup” is not part of the Monroney sticker. That’s the dealer adding margin. The Monroney sticker only contains manufacturer information. You can negotiate dealer-added items off the price.

The EPA Fuel Economy Label

The most useful number on the sticker that most buyers glance at and forget.

Every new car sold in the United States includes an EPA fuel economy estimate on the Monroney sticker. It shows city MPG, highway MPG, combined MPG, and an estimated annual fuel cost. These numbers come from standardized laboratory tests run by the Environmental Protection Agency — your real-world mileage will vary based on driving habits, terrain, weather, and how heavy your right foot is.

But even as estimates, these numbers are your best tool for comparing fuel costs between vehicles. The difference between 30 MPG and 40 MPG might not sound dramatic. Over a year? Over five years? It adds up fast.

Fuel economy differences compound over years. A 10 MPG difference over 5 years of ownership can mean $2,000+ in savings — before you even factor in rising fuel prices. Always check the EPA label and compare across the models you’re considering.

Decoding a VIN

Seventeen characters that tell you everything about where, when, and how a vehicle was built.

Every vehicle manufactured for sale in the United States has a 17-character Vehicle Identification Number. It is stamped on a metal plate at the base of the windshield (driver’s side), printed on the driver’s door jamb sticker, and recorded in the title, registration, and insurance documents. It is not random — each segment encodes specific information.

Position 1 Country of manufacture (1 = USA, 2 = Canada, J = Japan, K = Korea, W = Germany)
Positions 2–3 Manufacturer code (e.g., KM = Hyundai/Kia, 1G = General Motors, T3 = Toyota)
Positions 4–8 Vehicle attributes — engine type, body style, model, trim level, and restraint system
Position 9 Check digit — a mathematical formula verifies the entire VIN is legitimate (fraud prevention)
Position 10 Model year (e.g., R = 2024, S = 2025, T = 2026)
Position 11 Assembly plant — the specific factory where the vehicle was built
Positions 12–17 Production sequence number — the vehicle’s unique serial within that plant and model year

Before You Buy: Run the VIN

Always check a vehicle’s VIN before buying — especially used vehicles. NHTSA.gov/recalls lets you check for open safety recalls for free. For full history — accidents, title status (clean, salvage, flood), odometer readings, number of previous owners — use services like Carfax or AutoCheck. A $40 report can save you from a $4,000 mistake.

Dealer Add-Ons: The Sticker After the Sticker

The second sticker is where dealerships make their real margin.

Many dealers attach a second sticker — sometimes called an “addendum sticker” or “supplemental sticker” — next to the Monroney. This lists dealer-installed accessories with their own prices. These are not factory options. They were added by the dealership after the car arrived, often at a dramatic markup over the actual cost of the product or service.

Common examples include nitrogen tire fill, paint sealant, pinstripes, VIN etching, fabric protection, and window tinting. Some dealers pre-install these items on every car in their inventory so they can tell you they “come with the car.”

If a dealer says these items “can’t be removed,” they can. Every dealer-added accessory is negotiable. “I’d like the car without those add-ons” is a complete sentence. If they won’t budge, the dealer down the road will.

Check Yourself

Make sure you can read a sticker before you stand in front of one at a dealership.

A car’s Monroney sticker shows a base price of $27,500 and a total MSRP of $32,100. What accounts for the $4,600 difference?

Optional equipment packages and the destination/delivery charge. The Monroney sticker itemizes every factory-installed option and its individual price, plus the destination charge. Add them up — they’ll total the difference between the base price and total MSRP.

The dealer’s window has a second sticker showing $1,400 in “dealer accessories.” The sales associate says it comes standard on all their vehicles. What do you do?

Ask for the car without the addendum items, or negotiate the total price down by that amount. Dealer accessories are never “standard” — they’re added by the dealership for margin. If the associate says it can’t be removed, ask to speak with a manager, or move to another dealer.

Two identical cars at different dealers have the same MSRP but different “out-the-door” prices. Why?

Dealer fees, dealer-added accessories, and potentially different local tax calculations. The MSRP is set by the manufacturer and is the same everywhere. But documentation fees, dealer prep charges, and addendum accessories vary from dealer to dealer. Always compare the out-the-door number, not just the sticker price.

A VIN starts with “JHM.” What does this tell you?

J = manufactured in Japan. H = Honda. M = specific division (Acura). The first three characters of a VIN identify the country of manufacture, the manufacturer, and the division or vehicle type. This car was built in Japan by Honda’s Acura division.

Your Assignment: Decode the Sticker.

Find the Monroney sticker for your Portfolio vehicle and break down every line.

Worked Example — 2023 Toyota RAV4 LE (2.5L)

  • Base Price: $28,275
  • Options: Convenience Package ($1,545)
  • Destination & Delivery: $1,335
  • Total MSRP: $31,155
  • Dealer addendum: paint protection ($499), nitrogen ($199) = $698 in add-ons I would negotiate off
  • VIN: 2T3F1RFV8PW123456
  • → 2 = Canada, T3 = Toyota, F1RFV = RAV4 LE attributes, 8 = check digit, P = 2023, W = Woodstock ON plant
  1. Find the Monroney sticker (window sticker) for your Portfolio vehicle. For new cars: search “[Year] [Make] [Model] window sticker” or use the manufacturer’s “Build and Price” tool online. For used cars: search “[Year] [Make] [Model] original MSRP” or look for the original build sheet.
  2. Record: base price, each option/package and its cost, destination charge, and total MSRP. Identify what each line item is and whether it was a factory option or a dealer add-on.
  3. Decode your Portfolio vehicle’s VIN using the breakdown from this module. Record what each segment means — country of origin, manufacturer, vehicle attributes, model year, and assembly plant.
  4. Identify any features on the sticker that you consider essential vs. unnecessary. Would you pay extra for the option packages listed? Which would you skip? Explain your reasoning.
  5. Submit your sticker breakdown and VIN decode to Canvas. This becomes Page 2 of your Vehicle Ownership Portfolio.

The sticker tells you what the manufacturer thinks the car is worth. Your job is to decide what it’s worth to you.
