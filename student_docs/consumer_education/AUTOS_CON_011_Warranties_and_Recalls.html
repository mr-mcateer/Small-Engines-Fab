Free Repairs You Didn’t Know You Had

Manufacturers fix things for free all the time. Most owners never check.

↓

Millions of vehicles are driving around right now with open safety recalls. Free fixes — parts and labor, no charge — just waiting to be claimed. Meanwhile, people pay hundreds of dollars for repairs that would have been covered under warranty if they’d just checked first.

Warranties, recalls, and technical service bulletins are three systems that exist specifically to protect you as a vehicle owner. In Module 7, you learned that your owner’s manual is the single source of truth. Now you’ll learn about the other protections built into every vehicle purchase — the ones most people never look up. This module teaches you how to look, what you’re looking for, and how to use what you find.

Warranty Types

Every new car comes with multiple warranties. They cover different things for different lengths of time.

Bumper-to-Bumper

Covers almost everything on the vehicle except wear items (brakes, tires, wiper blades). Typically 3 years / 36,000 miles, whichever comes first. This is your broadest coverage.

3 yr / 36K mi (typical)

Powertrain

Covers engine, transmission, and drivetrain components — the most expensive things to repair. Typically 5 years / 60,000 miles. Some manufacturers offer 10 yr / 100K mi.

5 yr / 60K mi — 10 yr / 100K mi

Corrosion / Rust-Through

Covers perforation (rust that eats all the way through body panels). Varies widely by manufacturer — anywhere from 3 to 12 years. Surface rust is typically not covered.

3–12 yr (varies by mfr)

Federal Emissions

Required by law. Major emissions components (catalytic converter, ECU, onboard diagnostics) are covered for 8 years / 80,000 miles. This is a federal mandate — every manufacturer must honor it.

8 yr / 80K mi (federal law)
Key fact: Warranties follow the vehicle, not the owner. If you buy a used car that’s still within its warranty period, the remaining coverage generally transfers to you. One exception: some manufacturers (like Hyundai/Kia) reduce their extended powertrain warranty from 10yr/100K to 5yr/60K for second owners. Always check the original in-service date, current mileage, and transfer terms against the warranty documentation.

How to Check for Recalls

Five minutes online could save you hundreds — or keep you safe on the road.

A recall happens when a manufacturer or the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) determines that a vehicle has a safety defect or doesn’t meet federal safety standards. When a recall is issued, the manufacturer is legally required to fix the problem at no cost to you — parts and labor, completely free.

Go to NHTSA.gov/recalls
↓
Enter your 17-digit VIN
↓
Review any open recalls listed
↓
Call any authorized dealer — schedule the free repair

You can also check directly on your manufacturer’s website. Most have a recall lookup tool. Either way, all you need is your Vehicle Identification Number (VIN), found on your registration, insurance card, or the metal plate on the driver’s side of the dashboard where it meets the windshield.

This Is Free and Takes 5 Minutes

Recalls never expire. Even if the recall was issued 10 years ago, if the repair was never completed on your specific vehicle, the dealer must still fix it at no charge. You don’t need to be the original owner. You don’t need to have bought the car from that dealer. Any authorized dealer for your vehicle’s brand will perform the repair. Check your VIN today — you might be surprised at what’s waiting.

Technical Service Bulletins (TSBs)

Not a recall, but a known issue the manufacturer has acknowledged.

A Technical Service Bulletin is a notice from the manufacturer to its dealer network describing a known problem and the recommended fix. TSBs aren’t recalls — the manufacturer isn’t legally required to fix them for free. But they’re important because they tell you the manufacturer knows about the issue and has an official repair procedure.

Why this matters to you:

TSBs in Practice

  • Your car has a recurring issue — maybe a rough idle or a mysterious noise
  • Your local shop can’t figure it out or keeps trying different fixes
  • A TSB exists that describes your exact problem with the exact repair procedure
  • You bring the TSB number to the dealer — they fix it right the first time
  • Sometimes the dealer will cover the cost as a “goodwill repair,” especially if you ask

You can search for TSBs on the NHTSA website under “complaints” and “technical service bulletins,” or through manufacturer-specific forums where owners track known issues. When you’re getting a repair quote from any shop, it’s worth asking: “Is there a TSB for this issue?” A good mechanic will check.

Goodwill repairs: Even outside of warranty, if a known defect exists and a TSB has been issued, manufacturers sometimes cover the repair partially or fully — especially if you ask politely, escalate to a service manager, or contact the manufacturer’s customer service line directly. It never hurts to ask.

Lemon Laws

When a new car just won’t work right, the law is on your side.

Every state has some form of lemon law, and they exist to protect buyers who end up with a new vehicle that has a substantial defect the manufacturer can’t fix. Oregon’s lemon law applies when all of the following are true:

Oregon Lemon Law — Key Requirements

  • The vehicle is new and still under the manufacturer’s original warranty
  • There is a substantial defect that impairs use, safety, or market value
  • The manufacturer or dealer has been given a reasonable number of repair attempts
  • The defect has not been caused by owner abuse, neglect, or unauthorized modifications
  • Remedies may include a replacement vehicle or a full refund

What qualifies: persistent engine failures, transmission problems, brake system defects, electrical issues that affect safety — anything substantial that the dealer can’t resolve after multiple attempts. What doesn’t qualify: minor cosmetic issues, normal wear, problems caused by aftermarket modifications, or issues with used vehicles (Oregon’s lemon law applies to new vehicles only).

Document Everything in Writing

If you think you might have a lemon, documentation is everything. Keep every repair order. Keep every receipt. Write down the date, mileage, and description of the problem every time it occurs. Save all communication with the dealer and manufacturer. A lemon law claim lives or dies on your paper trail. Start documenting from day one — not after you’re frustrated.

Check Yourself

Make sure you’ve got the concepts before you move on.

You bought a 2-year-old used car with 28,000 miles. The original bumper-to-bumper warranty is 3 years / 36,000 miles. Are you still covered?

Yes. Warranties follow the vehicle, not the owner. You still have approximately one year and 8,000 miles of bumper-to-bumper coverage remaining. Check the original in-service date (not your purchase date) to confirm. The powertrain warranty likely extends even further — potentially another 3 years and 32,000 miles.

A recall was issued on your vehicle 6 years ago, but the previous owner never had it done. Can you still get it fixed for free?

Yes. Recalls do not expire. If the repair was never completed on your specific VIN, any authorized dealer must still perform the recall repair at no cost to you. It doesn’t matter when the recall was issued, who owned the car at the time, or where you bought it. Go to NHTSA.gov/recalls, enter your VIN, and schedule the repair.

Your car has a rough idle. Your mechanic can’t figure it out. A friend mentions TSBs. What should you do?

Search for a TSB related to your vehicle’s year, make, model, and symptom. Check the NHTSA website or manufacturer-specific forums. If a TSB exists, bring the bulletin number to a dealer — they’ll have the exact repair procedure. You can also ask about goodwill coverage, especially if the car is close to its warranty period. Even if you pay, the repair will likely be faster and more accurate with the TSB in hand.

Your brand-new car has been in the shop 4 times for the same engine problem, and it’s still not fixed. What law might protect you?

Your state’s lemon law. In Oregon, a new vehicle with a substantial defect that impairs use, safety, or value — and that the manufacturer can’t fix after a reasonable number of attempts — may qualify. Start documenting immediately: save every repair order, note dates and mileage, and keep all communication in writing. Contact the manufacturer’s customer service line and request a case number. You may be entitled to a replacement vehicle or full refund.

Your check engine light is on. A shop quotes $800 to replace the catalytic converter. The car is 7 years old with 72,000 miles. Should you pay?

Not yet. The federal emissions warranty covers major emissions components — including the catalytic converter — for 8 years / 80,000 miles. Your vehicle is still within that coverage. Take it to an authorized dealer and have them diagnose it under the emissions warranty. The repair should be free. Always check warranty coverage before paying for any major repair.

Your Assignment: Warranty & Recall Summary

Research the protections that exist for your portfolio vehicle.

  1. Go to NHTSA.gov/recalls and enter the VIN for your portfolio vehicle (or use the year/make/model search if you don’t have a specific VIN). Record:
    • Number of open recalls (if any)
    • Description of each recall and whether it’s been completed
    • If no open recalls, state that and note the total number of past recalls for the model
  2. Identify the warranty coverage periods for your portfolio vehicle. Look up the manufacturer’s warranty terms and record:
    • Bumper-to-bumper coverage (years / miles)
    • Powertrain coverage (years / miles)
    • Corrosion coverage (years / miles)
    • Federal emissions coverage (8 yr / 80K mi — confirm this applies)
    • Based on the vehicle’s age and mileage, note which warranties are still active
  3. Search for Technical Service Bulletins related to your portfolio vehicle. Check the NHTSA website or manufacturer forums. Record:
    • Number of TSBs found
    • Brief description of the most common or relevant ones
    • Any that relate to problems you’d want to watch for
  4. Create a one-page warranty summary document for your portfolio vehicle that includes all of the above. Format it clearly — this is something you’d keep in your glove box.
  5. Submit your warranty and recall summary to Canvas. This becomes Page 11 of your Vehicle Ownership Portfolio — Warranty & Recalls.

This is real information about a real vehicle. If you’re using a car your family currently owns, share the results with them — you might find an open recall they didn’t know about.

  1. Warranties follow the vehicle, not the owner — always check what coverage remains before paying for repairs.
  2. Recalls are free, they never expire, and checking takes five minutes at NHTSA.gov/recalls.
  3. TSBs and lemon laws are powerful tools most owners never use — because they never learn they exist.
