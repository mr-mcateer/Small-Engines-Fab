Every Mile Is on Your Record

Your driving record follows you for years. Every ticket, every accident, every choice behind the wheel has a price tag.

↓

Your driving record is a financial document. Insurance companies check it before they quote you a rate. Employers check it before they hand you the keys to a company vehicle. Landlords check it when you apply for housing. A single ticket can cost you hundreds per year in increased premiums — and those increases follow you for three to five years.

Most young drivers think of a traffic ticket as a one-time fine. Pay the court, move on, forget about it. But the court fine is the smallest part of the bill. The real cost lives in what your insurance company does with that information. Remember those premium factors from Module 4? A speeding ticket adds 10–30% to your rate for three to five years. This module breaks down the actual math so you can make informed choices every time you get behind the wheel.

The Real Cost of a Ticket

The fine is just the door charge. The insurance increase is the real bill.

What You Pay the Court

$160 Speeding fine · 15 mph over · One-time payment

What You Actually Pay

$1,960 $160 fine + $600/year insurance increase × 3 years
The multiplier effect: 12× the original fine.

Based on average Oregon insurance increase for a single speeding violation on a young driver’s policy.

A speeding ticket doesn’t just cost $160. It triggers a rate increase that compounds every month for years. Insurance companies use your driving record as a risk prediction tool. One violation tells them you’re statistically more likely to file a claim — and they price that risk into every premium you pay going forward.

Two violations in three years? You may lose your “good driver” discount entirely. Three or more? You might get dropped by your carrier and forced into high-risk insurance, where premiums can double or triple.

The math is brutal for young drivers. Insurance companies already charge higher premiums for drivers under 25. Adding violations on top of an already-expensive base rate means the percentage increase hits harder in real dollars. A 30% increase on a $200/month policy is $60/month — $720 per year — for one ticket.

DUI: The $10,000+ Mistake

This is the most expensive single decision you can make behind the wheel.

And that’s just the financial cost. A DUI conviction stays on your Oregon driving record for life. It shows up on background checks. It can disqualify you from jobs that require driving — delivery, sales, transportation, construction. Some professional licenses require disclosure. Some colleges ask about criminal convictions on applications.

The ignition interlock device means blowing into a breathalyzer every time you start your car — and at random intervals while driving. Everyone in the car sees it. Every passenger knows why it’s there.

This is 100% preventable. A $15 rideshare home from a party looks very different next to a $10,000+ DUI. Plan your ride before you need one. Designate a driver. Call someone. There is no scenario where driving impaired makes financial or personal sense.

How to Keep Your Record Clean

Clean record = lower insurance = more money in your pocket. Every year. For decades.

Speed Management

Set cruise control. Match traffic flow. Know that speed limits drop in construction zones, school zones, and residential areas. The 5-over mentality adds up — and Oregon officers absolutely write tickets for 5 over.

Phone Discipline

Phone goes in the glovebox or on Do Not Disturb before you start the engine. No exceptions. Not at a red light. Not “just for a second.” One glance down at 35 mph covers the length of a football field blind.

Defensive Driving

Scan intersections before entering. Maintain following distance (3–4 seconds minimum). Check mirrors every 5–8 seconds. Assume other drivers will do the wrong thing — because eventually, they will.

Never Drive Impaired

Alcohol, marijuana, prescription drugs, exhaustion — all of them impair your reaction time and judgment. Drowsy driving is as dangerous as drunk driving. If you’re too tired to focus, pull over.

Oregon Defensive Driving Course

If you do get a ticket, Oregon allows you to take a state-approved defensive driving course to dismiss it from your record — once every five years. The course costs around $30–50 and takes about four hours. It’s worth every minute compared to three years of insurance increases. But you only get this option once every five years, so don’t waste it on a small ticket if you can simply pay the fine and keep a clean record going forward.

Always wear your seatbelt. Oregon law requires it for all occupants. But beyond the $130 fine, an unbelted driver in a crash faces catastrophically higher medical costs and injury severity. The seatbelt isn’t just a law — it’s the single most effective piece of safety equipment in your vehicle.

Check Yourself

Make sure you understand the real costs before you move on.

You get a $160 speeding ticket. Your insurance goes up $50/month. What’s the total cost over 3 years?

$1,960. The $160 fine plus $50/month × 36 months = $1,800 in insurance increases. Total: $1,960. The court fine was only 8% of the real cost. The other 92% went to your insurance company.

You’re 17 and get pulled over while using your phone at a red light. Is that legal in Oregon?

No. Oregon law prohibits all mobile device use for drivers under 18 — including at red lights, including hands-free. The fine starts at $265 for a first offense, and the violation goes on your driving record where it affects your insurance rates when you start paying your own policy.

Your friend says a DUI only costs about $1,000. Is that accurate?

Not even close. The court fine alone may be $1,000, but the total cost includes SR-22 insurance ($1,500–$3,000/year extra for 3 years), ignition interlock ($100/month), attorney fees ($2,000–$5,000), treatment programs, and lost income during license suspension. Realistic total: $10,000–$20,000+ over three years. Your friend is looking at the first bill and ignoring the next dozen.

You already have one speeding ticket on your record and just got a second. How does your insurance respond?

It gets significantly worse. With two violations in three years, you’ll likely lose your good-driver discount entirely. Your rate increase compounds — the second violation typically triggers a larger percentage increase than the first. Some insurers may non-renew your policy, forcing you to shop for coverage at high-risk rates. You could be looking at 50–100% higher premiums than a clean-record driver.

You got a ticket for following too close. Can you take a defensive driving course to dismiss it?

Yes — if you haven’t used this option in the past five years. Oregon allows one traffic ticket dismissal through a state-approved defensive driving course every five years. The course costs about $30–50, takes four hours, and keeps the violation off your record. But think strategically — if this is a minor ticket, it might be worth saving that one-time option for a bigger violation later.

Your Assignment: Safe Driving Commitment

Research the real costs and make an informed commitment.

Worked Example — 3-Year Cost of a Speeding Ticket

  • Violation: 15 mph over the speed limit
  • Court fine: $160
  • Current insurance: $150/month ($1,800/year)
  • Insurance increase: +30% = $45/month additional
  • Duration of increase: 3 years (36 months)
  • Total insurance cost: $45 × 36 = $1,620
  • Grand total: $160 + $1,620 = $1,780
  • That’s 11× the original fine
  1. Research and list 5 common Oregon traffic violations with their base fines. Include: speeding, distracted driving (cell phone), DUI, running a red light, and one additional violation of your choice.
  2. For each violation, research the typical insurance premium increase (percentage and estimated dollar amount per month). Use your current or estimated insurance rate as a baseline.
  3. Pick one violation — speeding — and calculate the full 3-year cost:
    • Court fine
    • Monthly insurance increase × 36 months
    • Any additional costs (traffic school, court fees)
    • Total real cost of the ticket
  4. Write a Safe Driving Commitment (one paragraph, minimum 5 sentences). State the specific habits you will practice — speed management, phone discipline, seatbelt use, impairment avoidance — and explain why in financial terms.
  5. Submit to Canvas. This becomes Page 13 of your Vehicle Ownership Portfolio.
