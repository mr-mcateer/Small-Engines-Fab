## 1. CLUTCH vs TORQUE CONVERTER

### The Short Answer for a Drift Trike
**A torque converter is the strongly recommended choice for a drift trike.** Here is why:

A drift trike needs smooth, controllable power delivery at low speeds (for drift initiation and control), variable ratio engagement (so you are not dumping full power to the wheels instantly), and the ability to load the engine at low vehicle speeds without stalling. A torque converter provides all three. A centrifugal clutch is binary — it is either engaged or slipping, and when it is slipping it generates enormous heat. Under the repeated low-speed, high-load conditions of drifting, a centrifugal clutch will overheat and burn out quickly.

### Torque Converter Advantages for Drifting
- **Variable ratio:** At low vehicle speeds the converter provides a mechanical advantage (roughly 2:1 at stall), so the engine can spin at its powerband RPM while the wheels turn slowly. This is exactly what you want when initiating a drift — engine RPM is up, wheel speed is controllable.
- **No-stall operation:** You can hold the trike nearly stationary with the engine at full throttle; the converter simply slips. With a clutch you would burn it out.
- **Smooth engagement:** The belt-and-sheave design provides progressive engagement rather than the abrupt grab of a clutch.
- **Engine braking:** When you let off the throttle, the converter provides some engine braking, which helps modulate drift angle.

### Centrifugal Clutch Disadvantages for This Application
- **Heat buildup:** Drifting involves lots of partial engagement (low vehicle speed, high engine RPM). A clutch in this state generates extreme friction heat.
- **Short lifespan:** Under drift trike duty a centrifugal clutch may last only a few sessions before the shoes/drum wear out.
- **On/off engagement:** No variable ratio — once engaged, it is 1:1. All gear reduction must come from sprocket ratios alone.
- **When a clutch IS appropriate:** Centrifugal clutches work fine for constant-speed applications (mini bikes cruising on flat ground, go karts on smooth tracks) where the clutch is either fully disengaged or fully engaged, not spending time slipping.

### Specific Torque Converter Options

- **Belt:** Comet 203589A asymmetric belt (specific to 30 series)

#### Comet TAV2 — 40 Series
- **Application:** Designed for engines 8-18 HP
- **Belt:** Comet 203591A symmetric belt (wider and stronger than 30 series)

- **Driven bore:** Typically 1" bore with 1/4" keyway (fits on rear axle)

- **Chain pitch:** Uses #40/#41 chain on the driven unit
- **VERDICT FOR THIS BUILD: ACCEPTABLE WITH CAVEATS.** The 40 series handles the power level well, but the stock RPM rating (~6500) is below your 8000 RPM ceiling. Solutions: (1) use it and simply limit your top RPM to ~6500 via throttle discipline, or (2) upgrade to a higher-RPM-rated unit. #### Juggernaut Torque Converters
- **What they are:** Juggernaut is a brand name for heavy-duty torque converters marketed specifically for high-performance mini bike and go-kart applications. They are essentially beefed-up versions of the TAV2 concept.
- **Key features:** Stronger springs, hardened sheaves, better bearings, rated for higher RPM and HP than standard Comet units.
- **RPM rating:** Some Juggernaut units are rated to 7500+ RPM, which is closer to what you need.
- **Price:** Typically $130-$200+ for a complete kit, significantly more expensive than a standard 40 series.
- **VERDICT:** A good choice if budget allows. The higher RPM rating and heavier-duty construction are better matched to a de-governed, billet-flywheel 212. However, availability can be inconsistent — they tend to go in and out of stock.

#### Hilliard Flame Clutch
- **What it is:** The Hilliard Flame is a high-performance centrifugal clutch made in the USA. It is the gold standard for centrifugal clutches in karting and mini bike racing.
- **Key features:** Sintered metallic shoes (far more heat-resistant than standard organic shoes), precision-machined drum, available in various engagement RPM configurations.
- **Engagement RPM options:** Typically available in 3000, 3500, 4000, and 4200 RPM engagement configurations by changing shoe springs.
- **Bore:** 3/4" bore standard (fits Predator 212 crankshaft)
- **Sprocket:** Integral #35 chain sprocket, typically 10T or 12T
- **RPM rating:** Can handle 8000+ RPM
- **Price:** $80-$120 for the clutch alone (no driven sprocket or chain included)
- **VERDICT FOR DRIFT TRIKE: STILL NOT IDEAL.** Even though the Hilliard Flame is the best centrifugal clutch on the market and can handle the RPMs, it is still a centrifugal clutch with all the limitations described above. It has no variable ratio — it is either engaged or slipping, and under drift trike duty you will still generate a lot of heat during drift initiation. HOWEVER, if a student is absolutely set on a clutch for simplicity (fewer parts, lighter weight), the Hilliard Flame is the only clutch worth considering for this power level. A higher engagement RPM (4000+ RPM) is better for drifting because it keeps the engine in the powerband when the clutch grabs.

---

### Ideal Engagement RPM for Drifting

For a drift trike with a torque converter:
- **1600-2200 RPM engagement** is typical and works well. The converter handles the rest with its variable ratio.

For a drift trike with a centrifugal clutch (if you go that route):
- **3500-4200 RPM engagement** is better for drifting. A higher engagement RPM means the engine is already making decent power when the clutch grabs, giving you more immediate control. A low engagement RPM (like 1800 on a stock clutch) means the clutch grabs when the engine is making almost no power, leading to bog-down and poor response.

## 2. CHAIN AND SPROCKETS

### Chain Size Selection: #35 vs #40 vs #41

#### #35 Chain
- **Pitch:** 3/8" (0.375")
- **Roller width:** 3/16"
- **Tensile strength:** ~2,100 lbs
- **Weight:** Lightest of the three options
- **Application:** Light-duty, lower-power applications. Standard on stock Predator 212 engines and most mini bikes under 8 HP.
- **VERDICT:** Marginal for a de-governed 212 making 12-18 HP. The #35 chain can technically handle the load in a straight-line cruise, but the shock loads from drift trike use (sudden traction changes, slides, re-grips) can push it past its limits. If you use a centrifugal clutch with a #35 sprocket (like the Hilliard Flame), you can make it work but should carry spare chain and master links.

#### #40 Chain
- **Pitch:** 1/2" (0.500")
- **Roller width:** 5/16"
- **Tensile strength:** ~3,700 lbs
- **Weight:** Moderate
- **Application:** Medium-duty. This is the standard chain for most go-kart and drift trike builds in the 10-20 HP range.
- **VERDICT: THIS IS YOUR CHAIN.** The #40 chain is the correct choice for a de-governed Predator 212 drift trike. It has nearly double the tensile strength of #35, handles shock loads well, and is the chain pitch used by the Comet 40 series torque converter driven unit.

#### #41 Chain
- **Pitch:** 1/2" (0.500") — same as #40
- **Roller width:** 1/4" — NARROWER than #40
- **Tensile strength:** ~2,500 lbs — LESS than #40
- **Application:** #41 is a lightweight version of #40. Same pitch, same sprocket compatibility, but narrower and weaker.
- **VERDICT:** Acceptable but not ideal. #41 fits on #40 sprockets (same pitch), so it is technically interchangeable, but it is weaker. For a school project on a budget, if you find a great deal on #41 it will probably be fine, but #40 is the better choice for the added strength.

**KEY POINT: #40 and #41 use the same sprockets** (same 1/2" pitch). #35 uses different, smaller sprockets. If you choose a 40 series torque converter, you are committed to #40/#41 chain on the driven side.

---

### Drive Sprocket (Engine/Converter Side)

If using a **torque converter (40 series):**
- The driven unit of the torque converter has a built-in sprocket, typically **10T or 12T for #40/#41 chain**.
- You do not choose this sprocket separately — it comes with the converter driven unit.
- Most 40 series driven units come with a 10T or 12T #40 sprocket.

If using a **centrifugal clutch:**
- **Hilliard Flame:** Comes with an integral sprocket, typically **10T #35**.
- **Cheap clutches:** Usually come with a **10T or 12T #35 sprocket** pressed on.
- You can buy clutch drums with different sprocket sizes to change the ratio.

---

### Rear Axle Sprocket (Driven Side)

This is where you set your final drive ratio. The driven sprocket bolts or welds to the rear axle or a hub attached to it.

**Target Gear Ratios for Drift Trike:**

The gear ratio is: (Rear Sprocket Teeth) / (Drive Sprocket Teeth) x (Converter ratio if applicable)

For a drift trike, you want:
- **Enough torque to break the rear tires loose easily** (this is the whole point)
- **Reasonable top speed** (drift trikes are typically ridden at 15-30 mph)
- **The engine to operate in its powerband** during drifting

**With a 40 series torque converter (10T drive sprocket):**
- The converter itself provides up to ~2.7:1 ratio at low speed
- A **60T rear sprocket** gives a sprocket ratio of 6:1. Combined with converter at stall: ~16:1 overall. At top speed (converter 1:1): 6:1 overall.
- A **72T rear sprocket** gives 7.2:1. At stall: ~19:1 overall. At top speed: 7.2:1.
- **Recommendation: 60T to 72T rear sprocket** for drift trike use. This range provides excellent low-end torque for drift initiation while still allowing reasonable top speed.

**With a centrifugal clutch (10T drive, #35 chain):**
- No converter multiplication, so you need a higher sprocket ratio.
- A **65T to 80T rear sprocket** is typical for drift trikes with clutches.

**Common rear sprocket sizes and approximate prices:**
- 60T #40/#41 sprocket: $15-$30
- 72T #40/#41 sprocket: $18-$35
- These are usually flat plate sprockets with bolt holes, designed to mount to a hub or directly to a wheel hub adapter.

**Where to buy:**
- **Go Power Sports:** Carries a wide range of #40 and #35 sprockets from 10T to 80T+. Individual sprockets ~$10-$35 depending on size.
- **OMB Warehouse:** Good selection of axle sprockets.
- **BMI Karts:** Sprocket selection plus sprocket/hub combos.
- **Amazon:** Many generic sprockets available. Search "#40 chain sprocket 60 tooth" or "go kart rear sprocket #40."

---

### Chain Tensioner

**You absolutely need a chain tensioner.** The chain run from the torque converter driven unit to the rear axle sprocket will be long (typically 18-30 inches), and without a tensioner:
- The chain will whip and slap, especially under the variable-speed conditions of drifting
- Chain can jump off sprockets during slides (when rear axle is at an angle to the frame)
- Chain stretch over time causes slack

**Types of chain tensioners:**
1. **Spring-loaded roller tensioner:** A small roller on a spring arm that presses against the slack side of the chain. This is the most common type for go-karts and drift trikes.
   - Price: $10-$25
   - Available from all the vendors listed above
   - Make sure to get one rated for your chain size (#40 roller for #40 chain)

2. **Adjustable fixed tensioner:** A bolt/slot system that lets you move the axle or converter position to take up slack. Many drift trike frames incorporate this by making the axle mounting slots adjustable.
   - This is usually built into the frame design rather than a purchased part.

3. **Combination:** Use an adjustable axle position for gross adjustment and a spring-loaded roller for fine/dynamic tensioning. This is the best approach.

**Recommendation:** Build adjustable mounting slots for the rear axle into the frame (so the axle can slide back and forth about 1-2 inches to adjust chain tension), AND add a spring-loaded roller tensioner for #40 chain (~$12-$20 from Go Power Sports or Amazon).

## 3. REAR AXLE

### Live Axle vs Dead Axle

**LIVE AXLE is correct for a drift trike.** Here is the distinction:

- **Live axle:** The axle is keyed and both rear wheels are locked to it. When the axle turns, both wheels turn at the same speed. There is NO differential.
- **Dead axle:** The axle is stationary and the wheels spin on bearings around it. Power is delivered to only one wheel, or through a differential.

**Why live axle for drift trikes:**
- Both rear wheels are locked together, which means both break loose simultaneously during a drift. This gives predictable, controllable slides.
- A differential would allow one wheel to spin faster than the other, which creates unpredictable handling during drifts.
- Simpler construction — no differential, no independent wheel bearings, no CV joints.
- The "no differential" characteristic is actually a feature for drifting: it makes the rear end want to slide in corners, which is exactly the behavior you want.

---

### Axle Diameter

**1" keyed axle is the standard for this application.**

- **3/4" axle:** Too small for a drift trike with 12-18 HP. While 3/4" works for stock Predator 212 mini bikes (5-6 HP), the increased power and lateral loads of drifting can bend or twist a 3/4" axle.
- **1" axle with 1/4" keyway:** This is the standard for go-karts and drift trikes in the 10-20 HP range. The 1/4" keyway is important — it is how the sprocket, hubs, and other components lock to the axle and transmit torque.
- **1-1/4" axle:** Overkill for this application. Heavier and more expensive, and component availability (bearings, hubs, sprockets) is more limited.

**Material:** 1018 or 1045 cold-rolled steel is standard. 1045 is stronger. Most vendor-sold go-kart axles are 1018 CRS, which is adequate for this application.

**Keyway:** 1/4" x 1/4" keyway, typically cut along the full length of the axle (or at least at the locations where components mount). Keys are standard 1/4" square key stock, available at any hardware store or from McMaster-Carr.

---

### Axle Length

For a **30-36" rear track width** (wheel center to wheel center):

You need to account for:
- Track width (distance between wheel centers): 30-36"
- Wheel/hub width on each side: ~3-4" per side
- Pillow block bearing width on each side: ~2-3" per side
- Sprocket location: between one bearing and the frame
- Overhang past outer bearings: 1-2" per side for snap rings/collars

**Recommended axle length: 36" to 44"**

- For a 30" track: ~36-38" axle
- For a 36" track: ~40-44" axle

A common approach: **Buy a 44" axle and cut to length during assembly.** This gives you flexibility to adjust track width during the build. You can always cut shorter but cannot add length.

**Pricing:**
- 1" x 40" keyed axle: approximately $30-$50 from Go Power Sports, OMB Warehouse, or BMI Karts
- 1" x 44" keyed axle: approximately $35-$55
- Amazon options exist but quality/straightness varies — a bent axle is useless

**Pre-keyed vs cutting your own:** Buy a pre-keyed axle. Cutting a keyway requires a milling machine or broach, which adds unnecessary complexity. Pre-keyed axles from go-kart suppliers are inexpensive and ready to use.

---

### Pillow Block Bearings

**Size: 1" bore pillow block bearings (UCP205-16 is the standard designation)**

**Quantity: 2 bearings minimum** — one on each side of the axle, mounted to the frame. The bearings define the axle position and carry all the loads.

**Specifications:**
- Bore: 1" (to match 1" axle)
- Type: UCP205-16 (pillow block with set-screw locking)
- Set screws: The bearing has set screws that tighten against the axle to lock it in position and prevent the axle from sliding laterally.
- Mounting: Two-bolt base, typically 3-3/8" between bolt holes
- Load rating: Standard UCP205-16 bearings are rated for 2,000-3,000 lbs dynamic load, which is far more than adequate.

**Price: $8-$20 each** — these are very inexpensive.
- Amazon 2-packs of UCP205-16: approximately $15-$25 for the pair
- Go Power Sports: ~$10-$15 each
- OMB Warehouse: ~$10-$15 each
- McMaster-Carr: Higher quality but ~$20-$30 each

**Mounting considerations:**
- The pillow blocks bolt to flat mounting plates welded to the frame.
- For a drift trike, consider making the pillow block mounting adjustable (slotted bolt holes) so you can slide the axle forward/back for chain tension adjustment.
- Mount the bearings so the axle is level and perpendicular to the frame centerline. Misalignment will cause bearing wear and handling problems.

---

### Axle Keyway and Component Mounting

**Keyway:** 1/4" x 1/4" is standard for a 1" shaft. All components (sprocket hub, wheel hubs) will have matching 1/4" keyways.

**How components attach to the axle:**
1. **Key:** A 1/4" square key sits in the keyway, half in the axle and half in the component bore. This transmits torque.
2. **Set screws:** Most hubs have set screws that tighten against the axle (and often against the key) to prevent axial movement.
3. **Collars:** Shaft collars (1" bore, set-screw type) are placed on either side of components to prevent lateral sliding. Price: ~$3-$5 each.
4. **Snap rings:** If the axle has snap ring grooves, internal snap rings can locate components. Less common on go-kart axles.

**Assembly order on the axle (from left to right, typical):**
1. Snap ring or collar (left axle stop)
2. Left wheel hub (keyed)
3. Collar
4. Left pillow block bearing
5. Sprocket hub (keyed) — between left bearing and frame
6. Frame crossmember area
7. Right pillow block bearing
8. Collar
9. Right wheel hub (keyed)
10. Snap ring or collar (right axle stop)

---

### Hub Attachment Methods for Rear Wheels

Several options for attaching wheels to the live axle:

#### Option A: Go-Kart Hubs (Recommended)
- **What they are:** Machined steel or aluminum hubs with a 1" keyed bore on one side and a bolt pattern (typically 4-bolt on a specific bolt circle) on the other.
- **Common bolt patterns:** 4 bolts on 4" bolt circle (4/4"), 3 bolts on 3-1/4" bolt circle, or various metric patterns.
- **Price:** $15-$30 each
- **Vendors:** Go Power Sports, OMB Warehouse, BMI Karts, Amazon
- **KEY ISSUE FOR YOUR BUILD:** The bolt pattern on the hub must match the bolt pattern on your wheel. If the student found specific wheels (ECILKUC 10x4.5-5), the bolt pattern on those wheels may or may not match standard go-kart hubs. This is addressed in Section 4 below.

#### Option B: Weld-On Hubs
- A steel plate welded to the axle (or a hub welded to a plate) with holes drilled to match the wheel bolt pattern.
- Advantage: You can make ANY bolt pattern work.
- Disadvantage: Welding to the axle requires skill and introduces heat that can warp the axle. Better to weld a plate to a separate hub that is keyed to the axle.
- For a school fabrication class, this is a viable approach — you can machine/drill a custom bolt pattern on a plate that keys to the axle.

#### Option C: Clamp-On/Set-Screw Hubs
- Similar to Option A but use set screws only (no keyway). These can slip under high torque.
- NOT RECOMMENDED for a powered drift trike.

## 4. REAR WHEELS AND TIRES

### ECILKUC 10x4.5-5 Wheels (Student's Find)

**Tire size breakdown: 10 x 4.5 - 5**
- **10:** Overall diameter approximately 10 inches
- **4.5:** Tread width approximately 4.5 inches
- **5:** Fits a 5" diameter rim

This is a standard go-kart tire size. It is commonly used on the rear of racing and recreational go-karts.

**Are 10x4.5-5 appropriate for a drift trike?**

**YES, with caveats:**
- The size is good — 10" diameter provides reasonable ground clearance and rolling speed, 4.5" width gives a decent contact patch.
- These are pneumatic (air-filled) rubber tires, which provide grip. For a drift trike, you do NOT want grip on the rear — **you need to either sleeve them in PVC or replace them with slick/hard surfaces.**
- The 5" wheel/rim diameter is important for hub and bolt pattern compatibility (see below).

**Hub/Bolt Pattern Issue:**

This is the critical issue the student identified. Go-kart wheels come with various bolt patterns, and they must match the hubs on your axle. Common go-kart wheel bolt patterns include:

- 3-bolt on 2-5/8" or 2-3/4" bolt circle
- 4-bolt on 4" bolt circle
- 5-bolt patterns (less common)
- Some kart wheels have 3/8" bolt holes, others have 5/16"

The ECILKUC wheels on Amazon — you need to carefully check the listing for:
1. Number of bolt holes
2. Bolt circle diameter (PCD - pitch circle diameter)
3. Bolt hole diameter
4. Center bore diameter

**If the bolt pattern does not match available go-kart hubs**, the student has several options:
1. **Drill new holes in the wheel:** If the wheel is a steel or aluminum disc, you can drill new bolt holes to match your hub pattern. This is feasible in a fabrication class with a drill press. Mark carefully, center-punch, and drill to match the hub bolt circle.
2. **Make adapter plates:** Machine a flat plate with your hub bolt pattern on one side and the wheel bolt pattern on the other.
3. **Buy matching hubs:** If you can determine the wheel's bolt pattern, buy hubs that match. Many Amazon go-kart wheel listings specify "4-hole" or "3-hole" patterns.
4. **Buy wheels and hubs as a matched set:** Some vendors sell wheel-and-hub combos specifically for live axle go-karts.

**Recommendation:** Before buying the ECILKUC wheels, determine the exact bolt pattern from the listing or reviews. Then source hubs with a 1" keyed bore that match that pattern. If no matching hub exists at a reasonable price, plan to drill new holes in the fab shop.

### PVC Pipe Sleeves — The Standard Drift Trike Approach

**Yes, PVC pipe sleeves over pneumatic tires are the most common and proven method for drift trike rear wheels.** Here is how it works:

**Concept:** You inflate the pneumatic tire normally, then slide a section of PVC pipe over the tire. The PVC provides a hard, slippery contact surface that slides easily on pavement. The pneumatic tire inside provides cushioning and shock absorption.

**PVC Sizing for 10x4.5-5 tires:**
- Overall tire diameter: ~10"
- Overall tire width: ~4.5"
- You need PVC pipe with an inner diameter slightly smaller than the inflated tire outer diameter, so it stretches on tightly.
- **Typical PVC size: 10" Schedule 40 PVC pipe** — the inside diameter is approximately 10.02", which fits snugly over a 10" tire (which may actually measure 9.5-10" depending on inflation and brand).
- Cut the PVC to approximately 5-6" wide (slightly wider than the tire).

**How to install PVC sleeves:**
1. Deflate the tire slightly.
2. Slide the PVC pipe section over the tire (may need to use soapy water as lubricant or warm the PVC slightly with a heat gun to make it more flexible).
3. Re-inflate the tire so it presses firmly against the inside of the PVC.
4. Some builders add a bead of silicone or zip ties at the edges to prevent the PVC from sliding off laterally.

**PVC sleeve pros:**
- Cheap — a 2-foot section of 10" PVC pipe is ~$15-$30 at a hardware store, enough for both wheels
- Easy to replace when worn
- Smooth, consistent slide
- Pneumatic tire inside provides comfort
- The standard approach — well-proven by thousands of drift trike builds

**PVC sleeve cons:**
- Can crack if the trike hits a sharp edge or pothole
- Wears down over time (plan for replacement)
- Can become uneven/eccentric if not sized/installed properly
- Adds diameter to the wheel, changing your effective gear ratio slightly

**Schedule 40 vs Schedule 80 PVC:**
- Schedule 40 is standard wall thickness — this is what most drift trike builders use
- Schedule 80 is thicker wall — more durable but heavier and harder to install
- Recommendation: Start with Schedule 40 for ease of installation, move to Schedule 80 if they wear out too fast

### Alternative: Purpose-Built Drift Trike Wheels

**Hard plastic/polyethylene drift wheels** exist as aftermarket products, but they are less common and more expensive:

- **Dedicated drift trike sleeves:** Some manufacturers make purpose-built HDPE (high-density polyethylene) sleeves that fit standard go-kart wheels. These last longer than PVC and provide a more consistent slide surface. Price: $30-$60 per pair.
- **Solid polyethylene wheels:** Some drift trike builders use solid plastic caster-style wheels. These are extremely durable but provide a harsher ride (no pneumatic cushioning). They also tend to be noisier.
- **Barrel material:** Some builders use cut sections of HDPE barrel (food-grade barrels) as sleeves. These are tougher than PVC and slide well on pavement.

**For a school project, PVC pipe over pneumatic tires is the recommended approach.** It is inexpensive, easy to work with (can be cut with standard shop tools), and proven to work. Save the budget for more critical components like the torque converter and axle.

## 6. CRITICAL NOTES AND WARNINGS

### Safety
1. **The 10K billet flywheel stores significantly more energy than a stock flywheel.** In the event of a mechanical failure (chain break, sprocket failure), components will be moving with more kinetic energy. Consider a chain guard/case over the chain run.
2. **A de-governed Predator 212 making 12-18 HP on a drift trike is genuinely fast and potentially dangerous.** This is more power than many commercial drift trikes. Ensure the build includes adequate braking (front disc brake at minimum).
3. **Helmet, gloves, and protective gear are mandatory.** This is not optional for any motorized drift trike, especially one with this power level.
4. **Kill switch:** Wire a kill switch that the rider can reach easily. A tether-style kill switch (lanyard attached to rider's wrist) is ideal — if the rider falls off, the engine stops.

### Assembly Tips
1. **Align everything carefully.** The torque converter driver, driven unit, and rear axle sprocket must all be in the same plane. Misalignment causes chain derailment and premature wear.
2. **Use Loctite on all set screws.** Vibration will loosen set screws over time. Blue Loctite (medium strength) on hub and collar set screws.
3. **Check chain tension frequently** during break-in. New chains stretch.
4. **Grease the pillow block bearings** before first use and periodically thereafter.
5. **Carry spare master links and a chain breaker tool** when riding.

### Gear Ratio Calculation Reference

Final drive ratio = (Rear sprocket teeth / Drive sprocket teeth) x Converter ratio

With a 40 series torque converter (10T driven sprocket) and 60T rear sprocket:
- At engagement (converter ~2.7:1): 6.0 x 2.7 = **16.2:1**
- At full speed (converter 1:1): 6.0 x 1.0 = **6.0:1**

Estimated top speed with 10" rear wheels (effective ~11" with PVC sleeve):
- Wheel circumference: ~34.5 inches = ~2.88 feet
- At 6000 engine RPM, 1:1 converter ratio, 6:1 sprocket ratio:
  - Wheel RPM = 6000 / 6 = 1000 RPM
  - Speed = 1000 x 2.88 / 5280 x 60 = ~32.7 mph
- At 8000 engine RPM: ~43.6 mph (this is VERY fast for a drift trike — use caution)

Adjust rear sprocket size to change this range. A larger rear sprocket (72T) gives:
- At 6000 RPM, 1:1: Wheel RPM = 6000/7.2 = 833 RPM = ~27.3 mph
- This is a more reasonable top speed for a drift trike.
